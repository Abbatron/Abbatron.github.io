English throughout the IB

MYP

English is an important subject within the IB MYP curriculum, and is mainly assessed through the following four criteria. Each of them consists of 20 marks within the e-Assessment.

  • Criterion A - Knowledge, understanding, and interpretation. This criterion is based off of understanding various texts. It is usually assessed by the student needing to answer comprehension questions on a text, or write responses to questions about texts, which links neatly into both Criterion B and Criterion C. For example, a student could be asked to answer questions around the characters' motives, a sequence of events, or even about the author's intentions.
  • Criterion B - Organizing. This criterion is based off of the ability to organize one's writing well, and in accordance with the context given. For example, if a student was writing a comparative essay between two texts, it would be organized very differently than, for example, a poem.
  • Criterion C - Producing text. This criterion assesses the ability of the student to produce different texts. For example, the student could be asked to write a comparative essay between two texts, a story, a poem, and even an article. Usually, this is the main part of an english assessment. For example, in my own mocks, one of the tasks that had the most marks for criterion C was writing a fictional autobiographical text based on an image given.
  • Criterion D - Using language. This criterion is assessed through the way the student uses language with knowledge of his or her audience. For example, if the student is told to write a piece of writing with 7 year olds in mind, but writes with language more suited to a 12th-grader, then the student would not score very well on that question. This criterion is why it is important to write appropriately for your target audience, and not just focus on the structure of the writing.

Despite the potentially confusing nature of the assessment to those new to the MYP, it is a lot easier to understand than one might think. The MYP e-Assessment for English is 80 marks in total, 20 marks per criterion. Usually, it is important to understand both the composition and content of the text that the student needs to write, as both are very important in order to score as high as possible on the e-Assessment. In the marking of the English e-Assessment, the total number of marks are taken, out of 80, and ranges are given to give a grade from 1-7. Usually, the grades are given based on ranges decided by the teachers after careful consideration, based on the difficulty of the test, which is in turn usually based of the average scores of the students. For example, if everyone was to score above 50, scores below 50 could be graded as 5s, while if most people scored below 50, it could be graded as a 6, though this is unlikely.

DP

English remains an important subject in the IB Diploma programme, but it become significantly more complex. Now, instead of working towards acing a singular e-Assessment at the end of two years, the student needs to prepare to pass a series of papers.

  • Paper 1 - This paper focuses on understanding one text, in SL English, or two texts, in HL English, in 1 hour and 15 minutes or 2 hours and 15 minutes respectively. These texts can be extracts from anything, fictional or non-fictional. Usually, the student is then asked an open-ended question at the end of the text, to be answered with a 'guided analysis'. It is important, when answering this question, to consider three things. What the immediate answer to the question is, how you know this, and what the author's intentions were. It is also important to consider the effect of the same on the audience.
  • Paper 2 - Paper 2 gives the student up to 4 prompts. The student must choose one of these prompts, and using two texts that he or she has studied in class, must then write a 1000-1500 word comparative essay, comparing and contrasting the two texts in the context of the given prompt. In SL English, 1 and a half hours are given, while in HL English, 2 hours are given, as HL students can compare 2 or 3 texts, while SL students only need to do two.
  • The IOs - The IOs, or individual orals, are perhaps the easiest to understand out of all the parts of english. These simply consist of the student choosing between two texts that have been studied in class, and speaking for 10 minutes on the chosen topic. Then, the student needs to answer questions on that text for 5 minutes.
