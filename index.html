What is the IB?

What is the IB curriculum? This is a very important question that will be answered throughout this website. The IB curriculum is split into 4 main parts. PYP, or the Primary Years Programme, MYP, or the Middle Years Programme, and the DP itself, which stands for the diploma programme. There is also a fourth, called CP (career program), with elements of the DP along with a career-related core. Unfortunately, I will not discuss the CP much. The first two, the MYP and the PYP are intended to give students an adaptive, hands-on learning experience, through the exploration of concepts called global contexts. These are wide-reaching topics that can help students learn a lot about themselves, the world around them, and how what they do impacts other people. I will go into more detail about these global contexts here.

There are a few main projects that are vital to the curriculum. The main ones are the PYP exhibition, which is essentially a group project about a topic of the student’s choice, which can be anything from space exploration to animation. It aims to teach students about teamwork, research and presentation skills. These are all vital to the student’s further development and can help both in and out of the classroom. The student’s work is then graded, and weighs in on their report. Another important project is the personal project. This is an individual project that, similarly to the PYP exhibition, is unique to each student. For example, I personally chose to create this site in order to help new students to the IB curriculum, for my personal project. Other projects include remote-controlled airplanes, games, and many more. This project is based around three main sections, as shown on the official IB website. It requires planning, and a concrete process that the student can replicate and that shows effort and the skill of the student, a tangible product, and an evaluation on the project as a whole. It is usually graded by the student’s own school, by supervisors, based on both external criteria, and ones that the students themselves set, as each project is unique, though the IB will moderate this, ensuring fair and unbiased grades.

Finally, in years 12 and 13, the years where students prepare for passing the actual IB examination, beginning the IB diploma programme, or the IBDP, extended essays, TOK, and CAS are the main focuses outside of regular schoolwork. The extended essay is a 4,000-word essay on a research topic of the student's choice. It is completely independent, and no aid is given from the teachers other than to guide students through the overall process. The TOK exhibition is when a student chooses from 35 prompts given by the TOK organisation itself, and choose 3 objects. The student then needs to explain, through a written commentary, how that concept manifests in the real world. If you would like to learn about TOK, specifically, click here.
