Languages

Languages are a very large part of the MYP and DP parts of the IB curriculum. Personally, in my school, we needed to do 3 main languages, Arabic, English, and French in PYP, and in MYP we had a choice between French, Spanish, and Arabic, alongside English and Arabic according to the Ministry of Education curriculum, which is specific in the UAE. Normally, an IB student only needs to do either French, Spanish, or whatever other language is offered, along with English. Unfortunately, I don't know which language everyone will choose, but thankfully, for the chosen language, the criteria and the ways of testing in MYP and IB are structured the same, just with different content.

MYP

In the MYP, Language Acquisition subjects are assessed through formative and summative assessments. The formative assessments are use by teachers to get an idea of the students' progress and level of skill, while the summative assessments are used to actually take the grade of the student, from 1-8, and these are the ones that go on the report.

  • Criterion A - Comprehending Spoken and Visual Text. Thankfully, the Language Acquisition criteria are fairly easy to understand. Criterion A is all about understanding the spoken language. The tests for this criterion usually include a recording or video, which the student has to listen to, understand, and then answer questions on. Usually, the recording is usually of something like an interview or someone talking about their experiences, for example on holiday, about their opinion on something, etc. There are questions first directly about the text, for example, "What did Emily do for her holiday?", where the student simply has to give a short, one to two line answer. There are then grammar questions, and finally, there are long answer questions, requiring the student to write out a paragraph answering them.
  • Criterion B - Comprehending Written and Visual Text. This criterion is nearly identical to Criterion A, but it focuses on understanding a written text rather than a spoken one. The texts can be anything from a letter or a news article. Similarly to Criterion A, the student needs to answer questions directly referring to the text, questions on grammar, and then finally a long answer question at the end of the section.
  • Criterion C - Communicating in response to spoken and/or written and/or visual text. This criterion is all about how the student is able to verbally communicate in response to different stimuli. For example, the student could be asked to describe an image, and then discuss its link to the country of origin of the language the student is speaking, for example, how the weather in the photo is similar to the weather in France, for example. The student needs to then be able to hold a conversation with the examiner for up to 6 minutes, depending on the level of the student.
  • Criterion D - Using language in spoken and/or written form. While this criterion does technically include both spoken and written form, the vast majority of the time this criterion consistes of being able to write a text in response to a prompt. It is important, when writing this text, to consider the target audience, which is usually given, the language you use, and, of course, your grammar. The organization and structure of your text is also important. For example, if writing a news article, the title, date, and subheadings are important.

DP

Language Acquisition in DP is based around a series of papers, along with an oral assessment, similarly to how English works.

  • Paper 1 - Productive Skills. Paper 1 requires the student to choose a writing task from 3 given options, each from a different theme, and to use the text type given in the instructions. Students are given 1 hour and 15 minutes for this task, to write 250-400 words.
  • Paper 2 - Receptive Skills. This paper judges the ability of the student to understand the language, both spoken and written. The paper is 1 hour and 45 minutes. 45 minutes for listening comprehension and 1 hour for the reading comprehension. There are three recordings and three written texts.
  • Individual Orals - A conversation between the teacher and the student in the chosen language, based on a visual stimulus, usually an image. The student needs to talk for at least 15 minutes on the image. After this, the student and the teacher have a discussion on an additional, different theme. The student needs to talk for 5 minutes on the second topic, giving a total time of at least 20 minutes.
