Maths through the IB

Maths is one of my personal favourite subjects. It is also quite a complex subject, and the emphasis that the IB places on problem solving, and the way that the subject is structured, through the different criteria, could easily be confusing to students from other curriculums, so I am happy to be able to possibly help with this.

Disclaimer: In MYP, you are allowed to choose between higher level and lower level maths, but the only difference - in MYP at least - is more content in less time, so the following criteria apply to both.

MYP

  • Criterion A - Knowing and Understanding. Here, the students are assessed on how well they are able to select the correct way of solving problems, and how they can apply familiar concepts to try and solve unfamiliar ones. They should be able to apply the correct mathematics in order to get the correct answer. They should also be able to do so in many different contexts, both familiar and unfamiliar.
  • Criterion B - Investing Patterns. In this criterion, students should be able to recognize and correctly identify patterns, using mathematics if necessary to find complex patterns. Students need to be able to describe patterns as general rules that remain consistent with later findings.They also need to verify and justify that rule. Verifying that rule is as simple as double checking that rule with terms of that pattern that are not given, but that the student knows and is able to prove are correct. Justifying is slightly more complex, and evolves depending on what exactly the pattern and rule are. Justification is how the student can mathematically prove that their rule is correct, and their methods that they used to find the said rule are also correct.
  • Criterion C - Communicating. This criterion is simple. It just requires the student to show adequate working-out, to ensure that logical, correct methods were used, in order to support criterion A. It is also important because, if a student makes a mistake in the answer, but their calculations were correct, showing those calculations can sometimes get half a mark if the student was going to get none. It is important that the calculations are coherent, cohesive, and concise. Mathematical notation should be used correctly and accurately.
  • Criterion D - Applying mathematics in real world contexts. For this criterion, students need to be able to identify relevant parts of a problem presented to them, select appropriate mathematical strategies to approach solving the problem, apply said strategies, and be able to justify both the degree of accuracy to which they believe the problem is solved and the degree of accuracy to which they believe the solution actually makes sense in the context of the real-world problem.

There are actually two different types of maths available in the DP, Analysis and Approaches, or AA, and Applications and Interpretation, or AI. Both of these have a higher level (HL), and a standard level (SL). Simply put, HL maths is more complex and in depth than SL. SL maths usually teaches students what the concepts are, but HL maths goes much deeper, discussing how and why those concepts work, usually asking students to apply their learning to significantly more difficult problems.

DP - AI

AI maths is about the practical, real-world applications of certain parts of maths. This choice focuses more on things like statistics and distribution. This is usually a better choice for professions that require more the application of math rather than the theory behind it, like economics and management.

  • Paper 1 - Paper 1 is a 2-hour long short response paper, with questions being worth between 5 and 9 marks each. A calculator is allowed.
  • Paper 2 - Paper 2 is a 2-hour long extended answer exam. It requires the students to show deeper understanding of the topics. Questions are usually worth 14 to 18 marks.
  • Paper 3 (HL only) - Paper 3 consists of two long questions. The exam lasts for one hour. The questions explore two HL concepts in detail.
  • The IA - All students are required to submit an IA, or internal assessment, that consists of a research paper done on a topic that interests the student, allowing students to apply their learning to real world concepts that interest them.

DP - AA

AA maths is more focused on the theory behind maths, and is better for students that wish to go into professions such as engineering, data science, and machine learning.

  • Paper 1 - Paper 1 is a non calculator paper that lasts for 2 hours. It focuses on inquiry, reasoning, and algebraic manipulation. It consists of a short answer section and an extended answer section.
  • Paper 2 - Paper 2 is a 2 hour long exam, and calculators are allowed. Paper 2, unlike paper 1, has a focus on using technology to find answers to mathematical problems, and tests how the student interprets and uses their findings. It consists of a short answer section and an extended answer section.
  • Paper 3 (HL only) - Paper 3, similarly to Paper 3 in AIHL, is a 1 hour long exam that consists of 2 questions. The questions both place an emphasis on reasoning and inquiry.
  • The IA - The IA is the same concept as the AI IA, and is an opportunity for students to apply their learning to a topic that interests them and to learn more about it.
